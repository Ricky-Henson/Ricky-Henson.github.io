Soup Dumplings (小籠包)

The Xiaolongbao is a type of steamed bun or soup dumpling that originated in China, but the Din Tai Fung restaurant in Taiwan made it famous!

The dumplings are sold in almost every night market in Taiwan if you can't be bothered to wait in line for long hours at any of the chain restaurants. Raohe Night Market has a small shop near the ornate gates that sells it freshly cooked, and you'll have the opportunity to watch the men who prepare it.

If this is your first time eating this, note that it has hot soup 'inside,' so don't stuff it all into your mouth. You should take a small bite on the side to slowly drain the soup out before swallowing it all. Be sure to dip it in vinegar and fresh ginger that they usually provide.




The lu wei concept is a street food concept where you choose various ingredients (meat, vegetables, tofu, noodles) from a stall, and then the store chops them all up and cooks them in a delicious brine or broth.

In Raohe, you can usually spot these stalls easily since they're usually the only ones with tables in the middle of the night market (or you'll see customers picking ingredients from bowls).

Pig’s Blood Cake (豬血糕)

Pig's blood cake is a blood pudding served on a stick as street food in Taiwan. Its alternative name is black cake. It is made with steamed pork blood, sticky rice and then coated in peanut powder and coriander with dipping sauces. Pig's blood cake came from Fujian to Taiwan and then developed. It is eaten as a snack. It can also be cooked in a hot pot. It is served hot by street vendors who keep it warmed in a wooden box or metal steamer. A description from Seriouseats described it as cross between a rice cake and mochi.

As street food in Taiwan, pig's blood cake is blood pudding served on a stick. It is made by steaming pork blood with sticky rice, then coating it with peanut powder and coriander, and serving it with dipping sauces. The street vendors serve it hot by keeping it warm in a wooden box or a metal steamer. It can also be cooked in a hot pot.

Taiwanese Meatballs (肉圓)

The meatballs also called Bahwan is a characteristic street food in Taiwan. According to legend, Beidou Town, Changhua County is the original location where the meatballs come from at the end of the 19th century. Its name comes from its appearance, which is generally a translucent oblate shape with a diameter of about 6 to 8 cm. This meatballs is a ball of translucent dough stuffed with pork, bamboo, and shiitake mushrooms, then served in a bowl doused with savory sauce.




If you are celebrating Moon Festival with Taiwanese people, the chances are you will be eating plenty of Taiwanese Barbeque with them. However, if you visit the night markets, then you can sample these delicacies all year round. It is fairly simple to make. A BBQ stall will consist of a selection of meat, fish, vegetables, tofu, and other products. The key to good Taiwanese BBQ is the sauce. This will be different at every stall, but will usually consist of soy sauce, oyster sauce, sugar, and a unique combination of herbs and spices.

Stinky Tofu (臭豆腐)

Stinky tofu is a kind of fermented tofu, which has a highly unpleasant, pungent smell, especially when it is deep fried. In Chinese it’s called “chou dou fu“, 臭豆腐. This dish is mostly eaten as a quick snack. It’s a popular street food, typically cooked on the side of the road at small stalls, and it’s not commonly sold in restaurants. Stinky tofu is considered by many to be the national snack of Taiwan.

Stinky tofu in Raohe is often sold cold, steamed, stewed, or deep-fried. As it is stinky, be prepared that your breath!




Taiwanese beef noodle is a beef noodle soup dish originated in Taiwan. It is sometimes referred to as "Sichuan beef noodle soup" (Chinese: Sichuan beef noodle) in Taiwan. Although this usage can create confusion as Sichuan has its own versions of beef noodle soups which may be sold at Sichuanese restaurants under the same name. The beef is often stewed with the broth and simmered, sometimes for hours. In Taiwan, beef noodle vendors may also have optional, often cold side dishes, such as braised dried tofu, seaweed or pork intestine. Beef noodles are often served with suan cai on top, green onion and sometimes other vegetables in the soup as well.

Taiwanese Hamburger (割包)

In this steamed bread sandwich, braised pork belly and pickled cabbage are often used. Coriander and nuts are also commonly included. That's a lot better than a fast food burger. It's really filling and extremely tasty.




Lu Rou Fan is a Taiwanese pork rice bowl that you’re going to love. It’s easy to make but pays off big in terms of flavor, all in one bowl. It calls for pork belly diced into small pieces and browned. This gets braised in a broth made with soy sauce, along with crispy fried shallots and mushrooms, until the fat melts away, leaving the meat so tender that it melts in your mouth.

As the origin of the flavor, the sauce is the key to making minced pork rice. The most popular way of preparation seen in Taiwan is stir-frying the ground meat with sliced ​​shallot in oil, and then boiling it in soy sauce.

Lu Rou Fan (滷肉飯)




O-A-Mee Sua (蚵仔麵線)

The Taiwanese O-A-Mee Sua came up with the idea of adding oysters and braised pig intestines to this simple dish which made it wildly popular. Mee-sua is made of wheat flour. During the cooking process, starch is released into the soup which acts as a thickening agent. One of the version is made with bonito flakes, dried cuttle fish and chicken stock. Best way to enjoy this dish is to send it slurping into your mouth! For those who love vinegar, this goes really well with black vinegar!

To make Tongzai Migao, first stir-fry glutinous rice with mushrooms, shallots, soy sauce, salt, wine, oil, etc., and then put sliced ​​marinated eggs, sliced ​​meat or dried meat into a small cone, and then stir-fry, put glutinous rice, then steamed. After it is cooked, put it upside down on the plate, drizzle with sweet sauce or dried meat, add coriander and dried radish, and it becomes a tube-shaped rice cake. Green Onion Cakes (蔥抓餅)

This contains Green onion flatbreads pan fried, usually with egg and usually many optional accompaniments, including fresh basil, cheese, spicy sauce, corn, kimchi, and more. Also called scallion pancakes and cong you bing (蔥油餅).

The materials and methods of making green onion cakes are similar to those of scallion pancakes (蔥油餅), but the difference is that the crust of the pancakes has a puffy texture by hand or tools, and eggs or cheese can be added as fillings and seasonings.

The ingredients for green onion cakes include flour, green onions, sugar, salt, and sesame seeds are optional. First mix the flour with appropriate amount of sugar, salt and other seasonings, add water and knead it into a dough, then wait for about 30 minutes, then evenly distribute the relaxed dough into a dough of equal thickness, smear some oil, chopped green onion, sesame seeds, and then wait for about 30 minutes. For 30 minutes, at the end, the rods are cut into thin slices and fry in a pan until both sides are golden brown, pat the crust while it is still hot, and then grab it and eat it.




The oyster omelette is a dish of Hokkien and Teochew origin that is renowned for its savory flavor in its native Chaoshan and Minnan region, along with Taiwan and many parts of Southeast Asia such as the Philippines, Thailand, Malaysia and Singapore due to the influence of the Hokkien and Teochew diaspora. Variations of the dish exist in some southern regions of China.

The oyster omelette is a Taiwanese "night market favorite", and has constantly been ranked by many foreigners as the top dish from Taiwan. Its generous proportions and affordable price demonstrates the trait of night market cuisines.

Sticky Rice Dumplings (粽子)

Zongzi is tetrahedral bundles of sticky rice steamed in banana or lotus leaves, and usually containing shiitake mushroom, pork, egg yolk, or other ingredients. Also called rou zong (肉粽) when they contain meat. They are a dish associated with the Dragon Boat Festival, but also served from street stalls year-round.

About Taiwan Night Markets

Taiwan night markets are street markets that open from the evenings until midnight. They can be found across all areas in Taiwan—on old streets, around colleges, or in popular tourist areas.
They are mostly food or drink stalls. Night markets are great for families with kids, couples, friends, or any hungry person looking for a cheap and tasty meal!
